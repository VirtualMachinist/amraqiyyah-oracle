## 1. The centerpiece: the Kinship Inalienability Rule (proposed R-033)

**The Muskogean grammatical import.** In Muskogean languages, kin terms cannot stand alone — they are obligatorily possessed (*cvcke* "my-mother," never bare "mother"). Amraqiyyah imports this as a soft law:

> **A kin term denotes a relation, not a free-standing thing. In referential use it carries a possessor** (suffix or construct): *iška·ku* "my mama," *pokni·to* "his grandmother," *sanu na·yesatu·i* "the citizen's sibling." **Bare kin terms are reserved for three uses:** the role/category (*na·weladu* "the parent [as role]"), vocatives (*Iška!* "Mom!"), and dictionary citation.

Culturally: nobody's mother exists in the abstract — she is always somebody's mother. The grammar now says so.

## 2. The redemption: S-N

Doc A's root SN ("son, brother," claimed from "Semitic *ibn*") was sentenced to retirement as an English masquerade. **The sentence is overturned:** Egyptian **sn** means *brother*, and **snt** means *sister* — core, indisputable Egyptian vocabulary. Doc A's *san·u* had a true etymology it never knew. S-N returns as **sibling** with the genderless reading: *sanu* = sibling (unmarked), *sanut* = sister (marked) — the unmarked form is unmarked, not male, consistent with R-006.

This establishes the **kin -t convention**: female kin terms carry an *etymological* -t (Eg. *snt*, Ar. *bint*), a recognizable pattern without productive gender morphology: *sanut, saʾut, banut, ʿamut, ḫalut*.

## 4. Primary nouns (new lexical class, proposed)

*iška, ika, pokni, mafo, mama, dadi, ala, kaz* (and earlier *din, hawt*) are **primary nouns**: citation form is lexical, not template-derived (the *din* precedent, now formalized). They keep a root anchor for derivation (*yašika* "to nurture") and take the full nominal machinery: article, plural -āt (*iškāt* mothers, *pokniyāt* grandmothers, *saʾwāt* sons, *kazāt* cousins), possessives, case with sandhi.
