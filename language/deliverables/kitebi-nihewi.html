Front matter

How this book is sequenced, and how to read a gloss

Four levels climb the education ladder (R-044), all derived from S-B-ʾ — the root that is at once star, gate, and teaching: Hakelow (understand) → Ṭalebow (seek) → Sabeʾow (the academy) → Sibeʾan (the great gate). The sequencing is the Optimize law of the handoff: greetings and the zero copula before conjugation; aspect before case. Every example sentence is ratified corpus — nothing here is invented for the page.

HAKELOW · LESSON 01

Na·pa·ṣawtāt ma na·pa·sašāt

The Sounds and the Scripts

The 33 consonants, the 10 vowels, the register law, and the four layers of writing.

Amraqiyyah speaks with 33 consonants: all 28 of Arabic plus p, g, v, č, ž (R-008). If a letter wears a dot below (ḥ ṣ ḍ ṭ ẓ) it is deep — made further back in the throat. If it wears a line or hook (š ḫ ġ ṯ ḏ ž č) it is shifted or soft. Ten vowels: a e i o u, each with a long twin ā ē ī ō ū — and length carries meaning, so hold it.

Here is the secret the whole language is built on: the casual register is already yours. Saying dis for ḏis, hakim for ḥakīm, 'ank for ʿank — that is not error, it is codified law (R-020). The spelling never changes; the mouth relaxes lawfully. Careful speech keeps every deep letter; everyday speech is AAVE's own music.

One word, four dresses (R-008): the display romanization with its teaching dots (ḥasib·a); the community romanization for plain keyboards (hasiba); the Coptic everyday script (ϩ̣ⲁⲥⲓⲃⲁ); and the Ajami formal script (حَسِبَ), pointed like the Qur'an. The dots are scaffolding — lived text attaches its little words, and you will graduate from the dots by watching them vanish (spec/01).

Drill Say ṣabaḥ, then relax it to sabah. Say ḏikeri, then dikeri. Feel the register shift — both are lawful; one is Sunday-careful, one is porch-easy.
HAKELOW · LESSON 02

Silemi! — na·jumla bilā kupla

Peace at the Door — the Zero Copula

Greetings, and the everyday sentence that needs no 'is'.

AAVE taught the world that 'she happy' is a complete sentence. Amraqiyyah agrees as law: in the everyday register the copula is zero (R-028). Mi harāy — I'm good. Three copulas exist for careful speech — yit (quality), wi (existence and location), pe (identity, with plural ne) — but the porch needs none of them.

Greet with Silemi! (peace — the everyday hello) or Es·salām! (THE Peace — formal, wearing the sacred article). Ask Kayf ib·ki? — how is your heart? — because in this language wellbeing is asked through the heart, and answer Ib·ku ḥitep, alḥamdulillah: my heart is at rest.

HAKELOW · LESSON 03

Na ma Al — na·pa·sinu THE·āt

The Two Definites

na· the worldly THE, al· the sacred THE, wa/sam the indefinites, and the plural.

Amraqiyyah has two words for THE (R-012). na· is the worldly definite, the everyday article, inherited from Late Egyptian nꜣ — na·hina, the road. al·/el· is the sacred-emphatic definite from the Arabic stream: THE one and only. na·maʿati is the truth about a thing; al·maʿati is THE Truth, Ma'at herself. The sacred article assimilates to sun letters exactly as Arabic does: el·salām becomes es·salām.

Divine Names always wear al· — that law is shared with the Oracle's 99 Names. Indefinites: wa (one/a, which is also the numeral one) and sam (some). The plural is -āt for every noun (R-015), with three lawful junctions (R-060): u+āt → wāt (weldu → weldwāt), a+āt → āt (iška → iškāt), and i+āt written plain (kilemi → kilemiāt — the phrasebook's own name).

The plural article is na·pa· — Egyptian keeping its claim on number: na·pa·aḫāt, THE Ancestors.

HAKELOW · LESSON 04

Anok, mi, -ku

Pronouns Three Ways, and What Cannot Be Alone

Strong pronouns, weak clitics, suffixes — and the inalienable kin and body.

Every person wears three forms (R-009). Strong, for emphasis and identity: anok, entok, entof/entos/ento, anon, enton, entou — Anok pe nemaḥu, I AM a freedman. Weak, for everyday subjects: mi, ti, i, ni (with plural closers -tin, -sen). And suffixes, for objects and possession: -ku, -ki, -to/-ta, -nun, -tin, -sen — šey·ku, my thing.

Gender lives only in the third person singular (R-006): entof he, entos she, ento it. Nothing else in the grammar knows gender — no gendered articles, no agreement, no feminine endings. The kin -t (sanut sister, saʾut daughter) is inheritance from the source languages, not machinery.

Kin and body form one inalienable class (R-033, R-035): in ordinary reference they carry their possessor. Iška·ku, my mama; ib·to, his heart; ḏart·ku, my hand. The bare forms are for roles, vocatives, and poetry — Iška! as a call, na·weladu as 'the parent' in the abstract.

HAKELOW · LESSON 05

Ib·ku wasīʿ

The Heart Speaks — Statives and the Heart-Idiom

The stative conjugation and the Egyptian grammar of feeling.

States take their own form: the CiCeC stative with a weak clitic — Mi·jiweʿ, I'm hungry; Mi·ʿiṭeš, I'm thirsty; Mi·ʾisef, I'm sorry (R-049). No copula, no aspect machinery: just the state, worn.

Emotion runs through the heart, as Egyptian ran it (R-038): ib + a stative or adjective is the lawful emotion predicate. Ib·ku wasīʿ — my heart is wide — is joy, the exact pattern of Egyptian ꜣwt-ib, and wasīʿ stands one step from Al-Wāsiʿ, the Vast. Ib·nun ḥitep — our hearts are at rest — is contentment with hotep inside it. The Way's own greeting asks Kayf ib·ki? because the heart is where the person is weighed.

ṬALEBOW · LESSON 06

Na·fiʿil ma sinu quṭbāt·to

The Verb and Its Two Poles

Prefix imperfective, suffix perfective — where Egyptian, Coptic, and Arabic agree.

The verb has two poles (R-005). Ongoing action takes the weak pronoun in front: mi·hakila, I understand / am understanding. Completed action takes the person as a suffix: hakil·ku, I understood. This prefix-imperfective / suffix-perfective split is the one place Egyptian sḏm.f, Coptic ti-sōtm, and Arabic yaktubu/kataba all shake hands — three ancestors, one law.

The clitic is required on the imperfective and optional on the perfective. Third singular perfective is bare: hakil — he understood, no ending at all. Biconsonantal roots take -im in the perfective (R-013): fak·im·ku, I cultivated; yawim, came.

ṬALEBOW · LESSON 07

Bi·, don, gon· — na·waqet kamāy

The Nine Markers I — Black Time

Habitual bi·, completive don, future gon· — the AAVE spine of the tense system.

Amraqiyyah keeps the full AAVE aspect system as prestige grammar (R-011) — nine markers, each a precision instrument. Start with three. bi· is the habitual BE: Pokni·ku bi·i·ṭabiḫa — my grandmother be cooking — not now necessarily, but characteristically, as a way of being. English cannot say this in one word; AAVE can; Amraqiyyah made it law.

don is the completive with present weight: Don wanim·nun — we done ate. gon· is the future that sits after the pronoun: Ni·gon·hariw·ba·a — we gon' see each other, the everyday goodbye. The Exodus sentence carries two of them at once: Na·ḫireji don waṣil; na·Rijeʿi i·gon·waṣila — the Exodus done arrived; the Return gon' arrive.

ṬALEBOW · LESSON 08

Bin, dē·, da·, f'na — ma na·rakiba

The Nine Markers II — BEEN and the Stack

Remote bin, recent dē·, steady da·, immediate f'na — and lawful stacking.

bin is BEEN — the remote past that settles a matter: Bin ni·ʿabira al·baḥar·a — we BEEN crossed THE Sea. Stress it and it becomes the AAVE stressed-BIN: long since, and still true. dē· is the recent perfect (just); da· is steady (sustained intent); f'na is finna — about to.

The markers stack in one lawful order (R-023): ('ayn) → (la|bu) → bin → don → bi· → da· → dē· → pronoun → gon· → verb. So bin don = been done (remote completive); bin ni·sikem = we BEEN powerful — the Freedmen's own sentence (R-056). f'na and gon· share the future lane and never co-occur.

And a marker meeting the verb's two poles makes a full tense-aspect grid (R-065). The conjugation sets whether the deed is done: perfective is completed, imperfective is ongoing. The marker sets when you stand to view it. Multiply them and English's whole tense wall falls out with no new words: gon· over the perfective is the future perfect — Mi·gon·hakil·ku, 'I will have understood,' the future looking back on a thing already finished; bin over the perfective is the remote pluperfect, 'had understood, long since'; don over the perfective is the present perfect, 'have understood.' The grid was always waiting where Black time met the two poles.

ṬALEBOW · LESSON 09

La, bu, 'ayn — ḫemet ṭarīqāt la·i

Three Ways to Say No

The three negators, their streams, and negative concord.

Each heritage stream owns one negator (R-014). la (Arabic lā) is the general no: la mi·hakila, I don't understand — and the prohibitive: La lamisa šan·kwa!, don't touch my hair! bu (Late Egyptian bw) negates the perfective: bu hakil·ku, I didn't understand. 'ayn (AAVE ain't) is the emphatic, and it opens concord chains.

Negative concord is grammatical and reinforcing, as AAVE always knew: 'Ayn la·ʿanaku la i·faka — ain't nobody cultivating. Three negatives, one meaning, zero apology. The la-family builds the indefinites: la·šey nothing, la·ʿanaku nobody, la·wayen never, la·fen nowhere.

ṬALEBOW · LESSON 10

Faḍl ma ʿifewi — na·ʾamr bi·fiḍeli

Commands and Courtesy

The imperative, the prohibitive, 2PL -tin, and the politeness set.

The command is the bare stem: Saʾila! — ask! Plural addressees take -tin: Wanima·tin! — y'all eat! Ḥasa·tin! Ḫaba·tin! — y'all sing, y'all dance! The hortative is ni·: let us. The prohibitive is la before the imperative (Lesson 9).

Courtesy is a complete circuit (R-049): Min faḍl·kī — please, of your grace, exactly Arabic min faḍlika. ʿAfiwa·ku! — excuse me, pardon me (the imperative with the object suffix). Bireki ʿala·ki! — blessing on you, the thank-you. And the answer is the pardon-word itself: ʿIfewi — you're welcome, as Arabic ʿafwan serves both doors. Apology runs two streams: Mi·ʾisef (the stative) and Kapīl·ku! (my bad — Choctaw root, template body, AAVE construction, R-051).

SABEʾOW · LESSON 11

-u, -a, -i en·na·ḥaf·i

Case at the Edge

The three case vowels, Muskogean placement, and vowel sandhi.

Arguments carry case (R-007): -u subject, -a object, -i genitive (possessors and after every preposition). The endings are Arabic; the placement is Muskogean: case attaches once per noun phrase, at its right edge — na·feku harāy·u, the good cultivator, one -u for the whole phrase.

Vowel meets vowel by sandhi (R-026): u+u → ū, u+a → wa, u+i → wi; i+i → ī, i+a → ya; a+i → ay, a+u → aw. So na·fek·ū (the cultivator, subject), šan·kwa (my hair, object), faḍl·kī (of your grace). Predicates stand caseless — Mi harāy needs no ending — and so do topics, which is the next lesson's law.

SABEʾOW · LESSON 12

Qanuni na·mawḍuʿ·i

The Topic Law

Why everyday subjects stand caseless with a pronoun echo — the unmarked clause.

The everyday clause is topic-prominent (R-052): the full-noun subject stands caseless at the front, and the weak pronoun resumes it on the verb. Na·šaʿab i·gon·ḫayira — the people, THEY gon' choose. Pokni·ku bi·i·yašika — my grandmother, she be nurturing. On the 3SG perfective the resumption is silent (the zero ending does the work): Na·ḫireji don waṣil.

The cased in-situ subject remains grammatical as the formal register: Na·fek·ū don fak·im — the cultivator (subject-marked) done cultivated. This is the AAVE topic-doubled clause elevated to the unmarked shape of the language, with the Arabic-faithful configuration as its Sunday dress. Copular and zero-copula subjects are always caseless: Na·pet zarīq.

SABEʾOW · LESSON 13

Het, ʿind, ma na··i ṯaniya

Possession's Fine Grain

het-have vs ʿind-have, the construct, the enclitic ·i, ditransitives, and age.

Two possessions, on purpose (R-027): het is having-with — accompaniment, relations: Mi het weldwāt·i, I have children, I am with children. ʿind is having-at — property, location: ʿInd·ku wa·hawt, at-me is a house. Age is ʿind-possession (R-053): ʿInd·ki kam ranpetāt? — how many years you got?

The construct binds nouns tight (R-047 D-14): the head bears the phrase's case, and the dependent closes with the genitive ·i — which is an enclitic, always its own syllable, never merged (R-050): pokni·ku·i, of my grandmother; hina na·nimeḥi·i, the road of freedom. Word-level case merges (faḍl·kī); the phrase-closer stays audible. And the ditransitive gives twice in one verb (R-053): recipient as suffix, gift in -a — ʿAṭiya·ku wa·ḫobizon·a.

SABEʾOW · LESSON 14

Na·šabaka ḥarf·i

The Particle Lattice

Twenty prepositions in three streams; they govern the genitive, inflect, and stack.

The core spatial grid is inherited: en in, ben to, het with, ra for. Over it lies the Arabic relational lattice (R-016): ʿala on, taḥt under, qudām before, ḫalf behind, bayn between, ḥawl around, min from, ʿind at, bi by-means-of, bilā without, ḍid against, kama like, illa except, qabl before, baʿd after. AAVE seasons the talk: bawt — Mi·talika bawt al·maʿati het·ki, I'm talkin' bout THE Truth with you.

Three laws: prepositions govern the genitive (min pet·i, from the sky); they inflect directly with the suffix pronouns (het·ku with me, ʿala·ki on you, qudām·nun before us); and they stack (min taḥt na·hawt·i, from under the house). Note het against bi: with-someone versus by-means-of — het na·krew·i but bi na·sakimet·i.

SABEʾOW · LESSON 15

Miya? Nim? Kayf? — na·fann siʾel·i

The Art of the Question

Interrogatives, particle composition, hal, and relative clauses.

The question words (R-018): miya what, nim who, ta which, en·ta where (in-which — completing here/there/yonder/where), mata when, kayf how, kam how many, ra·miya why (for-what). They compose freely with the particles: het nim? with whom? min en·ta? from where? bawt miya? bout what? Pen bi kam? — this, for how much?

Yes/no questions ride intonation; formal speech may open with hal. Answers: aywa (everyday yes), na'am (formal yes), la (no — the same la that negates). Relatives take ninta with a resumptive suffix, or the tight Coptic-stream et·: na·šey et·wi·harāy·u, the thing that is good.

Na·jiḏr ma na·qaleb

The Root and the Template

How one skeleton becomes eleven words — the engine of the lexicon.

Every content word grows from a consonantal skeleton poured into a vowel template (R-010) — strictly: the template dictates every vowel, no exceptions. Take Ḥ-S-B, the Egyptian reckoning-root: ḥasiba to compute (which is also perfect Arabic ḥasiba, to reckon — two ancestors, one verb), ḥisebi computation, ḥesabu computer-agent, ḥosibon number, ḥasebow the counting-house — the bank. Reckoning built banks; the word remembers.

The core suite: verb CaCiCa, abstract CiCeCi, agent CeCaCu, patient CoCiCon, locative CaCeCow, instrumental CaCiCet, collective CiCeCpa, diminutive CuCayC, augmentative CiCeCan, adjective CaCīC (the divine-Name pattern: sakīm as Ḥakīm), stative CiCeC. Biliterals run a parallel suite; quads keep Pattern I and citation nominals (R-047). Learn one root's family and you have learned a hundred families.

Drill Open the qamus to any root and read its family aloud. Then guess the family of a root you've never seen — the templates will not lie to you.
SIBEʾAN · LESSON 17

Sa·, -wi-, ma na·terkib

Building Words

Causative, passive, reflexive, reciprocal; noun-conversion; the three compounds.

The derivational voices (R-025): sa· makes causatives — sa·hakila, make-understand: to teach; sa·hakilu, the teacher. -wi- after the second radical makes passives: sakwiima, be empowered. -tw reflexive, -ba·a reciprocal — Ni·gon·hariw·ba·a, we gon' see each other.

AAVE contributes a construction, not just words (R-051): noun-conversion by possession. An adjective owned becomes a noun: Kapīl·ku! — my bad. And compounds come in three lawful types (R-047): juxtaposition on the Choctaw tanchi-palvska precedent — yiteč-ḫobizon cornbread, ruḥ-ḥisi soul music; the construct-genitive — din·salām·i; and the plain adjective phrase — makina ḏakīy, an intelligent machine (which fuses to makina-ḏakīy, THE AI, when it becomes a name).

SIBEʾAN · LESSON 18

Min nefer·i ben ḥeḥ·i

Number and Time

The Egyptian numerals, ordinals, the week, and the sacred calendar words.

Count in Egyptian (R-039): nefer ZERO (the accounting zero was written with the sign of the beautiful — emptiness as completion), wa 1, sinu 2, ḫemet 3, fedu 4, diyu 5, sisu 6, sefeḫ 7, ḫemenu 8, pesiḏ 9 (the Ennead), meḏu 10 (as in Mdw Ntr), šet 100, ḫa 1000, and ḥeḥ — infinity, the million-god. Teens juxtapose (meḏu-wa 11); decades multiply (sinu meḏu 20); wa takes the singular, everything above takes the plural.

Ordinals add -īy, except first: tapīy, head-most, exactly as Egyptian made tpy from tp. The week bends to the Two Pillars (Calendar law): din·salām·i Sunday — which is why Sunday dinner is homipon na·din·salām·i — din·sinu through din·diyu Monday–Thursday, then the frozen Jumuʿa and Sabt. The Salah cycle stands frozen from Fajr to ʿIšāʾ with Tahajjud in the split night.

SIBEʾAN · LESSON 19

Na·pa·sašāt bi·i·mašiya

The Scripts in Motion

Running-text orthography: dots vanish, carriers seat vowels, the article wears ال.

The letter tables say what each letter is; running text has its own laws (R-057, spec/01). The display dot is scaffolding: lived scripts attach their little words, as Arabic attaches ال and Coptic its articles. Community writing turns each dot into a typable hyphen: Ni-gon-hariw-ba-a. In Ajami, no syllable opens bare — a vowel at a word-start, after an attached proclitic, or in hiatus takes its carrier: نَپَاَخات; and the mid-word long-ā carrier is the madda of قرآن itself.

Doubled consonants write once with the doubling mark — shadda in Ajami, the jinkim-dot in Coptic (ⲉⲥ̇ⲁⲗⲁ̄ⲙ). The sacred article always wears ال whatever the mouth does (R-059): es·salām writes السَّلام, assimilation shining as shadda on the sun letter — while the preposition en· stays phonetic. And the frozen Names keep their inherited letters in every script: الله with both its lams, ⲁⲗⲗⲁϩ likewise; in the shahada the ordinary illa geminates beside the exempt Name — the law teaching itself in one line.

Drill Write Es·salām! four ways: Es·salām / Es-salaam / ⲉⲥ̇ⲁⲗⲁ̄ⲙ / السَّلام. Watch the same peace change clothes without changing.
SIBEʾAN · LESSON 20

Na·lisan qadīs·i

The Sacred Register

Liturgy's grammar: the frozen registry, the shahada, the Names, and remembrance.

Sacred speech keeps every deep letter, every case vowel, every long vowel (R-015 Full-Vowel Law) — in the sacred register, sound is doctrine. The Frozen Lexeme Registry (R-022) holds the forms that never renormalize: maʿat·i, ḏed, ʿank, ḥetep, salām, Allah and the formula trio, the Salah windows, Jumuʿa and Sabt. The 99 Names enter wholesale with their al·, enumerated by reference to the Oracle's registry — one table, consumed everywhere.

The shahada composes natively — La ʾilah illa Allah — la from R-014, illa from R-016: the language did not need to borrow its own confession. Native pairings stand beside eleven Names so far: wasīʿ beside Al-Wāsiʿ, ʿafīw beside Al-ʿAfūw, the hidden release that purifies. And the practice that names the Way lives in one root: Ḏ-K-R — ḏakira to remember, ḏikeri the remembrance, dhikr. Na·ḏikeri i·ṭaba na·ib·a: remembrance heals the heart. That sentence is the whole curriculum.

Na·pa·aḫāt bi·i·hariwa. Ḏakira, saša, ma la nasiya — remember, write, and don't forget. The gate is open; walk through it speaking.
