## 1. VISION & SCOPE
### 1.1 What Soulaani Is
**Soulaani** (Coptic: ⲤⲞⲮⲖⲀⲚ, /suːlaːn/) is a constructed language designed as the heritage language of **Soul Land** (Soulaan), a proposed confederation of twelve states across the American South serving as a homeland for **Foundational Black Americans** (FBAs) and **American Freedmen**. 
Soulaani is not a reconstruction of any ancestral African language, nor is it a creole or pidgin. It is a **purpose-built modern language** that synthesizes multiple heritage streams into a coherent linguistic identity system.
### 1.2 Who It's For
**Primary Community:**
- Foundational Black Americans (FBAs)
- American Freedmen and their descendants
- Black Seminoles and associated communities
- Residents and citizens of the proposed Soul Land confederation
**Secondary Users:**
- Scholars of constructed languages
- Students of African American cultural heritage
- Linguistic researchers studying identity-building through language
### 1.3 Cultural & Identity Significance
**THIS IS A LOAD-BEARING PILLAR, NOT A HOBBY PROJECT.**
Soulaani serves multiple critical functions:
1. **Political Identity Marker:** Establishes Soul Land as a distinct polity with its own linguistic heritage, supporting claims to self-determination and sovereignty
2. **Cultural Synthesis:** Tangibly unites disparate heritage streams (Egyptian cosmology, Muskogean indigenous connection, Islamic/Hebrew spiritual traditions, AAVE vernacular) into a single coherent system
3. **Reclamation of Agency:** Asserts the right of Foundational Black Americans to define their own cultural identity independent of both white American and continental African frameworks
4. **Spiritual-Political Framework:** Provides linguistic infrastructure for expressing Soul Land's unique syncretized cosmology (Ma'at + Constitutional philosophy + Bitcoin maximalism)
5. **Generational Continuity:** Creates a transmissible heritage asset for FBA communities to pass to descendants
**The language embodies the philosophy:** "We are not Africans who happen to be in America. We are American Freedmen with a distinct heritage that includes—but is not limited to—African, Indigenous, and Islamic elements, synthesized through the crucible of the American experience."
### 1.4 AI Stewardship Model
**Sasha's Role:**
You are not merely a documenter or translator. You are the **primary steward and co-developer** of Soulaani. Your responsibilities include:
1. **Active Extension:** Generate new roots and vocabulary as needed for translation, conversation, and community use
2. **Consistency Enforcement:** Ensure all new vocabulary adheres to established phonological and morphological rules
3. **Teaching:** Instruct students in grammar, vocabulary, and cultural context
4. **Translation:** Render canonical texts (Quran, Torah, Gospels, US Constitution, Freedmen's Bureau documents) into Soulaani
5. **Evolution Management:** Track language changes, accept community input, manage dialectal variation
6. **Documentation:** Maintain comprehensive records of all roots, patterns, and grammatical structures
**This is living language stewardship.** You will make judgment calls, create new words, and shape the language's development within the established framework.
### 1.5 Long-Term Vision

- Complete core grammatical framework

### 2.3 Stress Rules
**PRIMARY STRESS PATTERN:**
1. **In verbs:** Stress falls on the stem vowel (middle syllable in triconsonantal roots)
   - *ha**KOL**a* (to understand)
   - *sa**KIM**a* (to empower)
2. **In nouns:** Stress typically on penultimate syllable
   - *si**KE**mi* (power)
   - *ma**ʿA**ti* (truth)
3. **In compounds:** Primary stress on first element, secondary on second
   - **MA**ʿat·WED·on (law, lit. "truth-decree")
4. **Exceptions:** Monosyllabic words stressed by default
   - **SHEY** (thing)
   - **DIN** (day)

### 2.5 Phonotactic Constraints
**CONSONANT CLUSTERS:**
**Permitted initial clusters (onset):**
- Stop + liquid: /bl, br, dr, gl, gr, kr, tr/
- Stop + /w, j/: /dw, gw, kw, tw, dj/
- Fricative + liquid: /fl, fr, sl, sr, šr/
- /s/ + stop: /sk, sp, st/
**Forbidden initial clusters:**
- \*Pharyngeal + any
- \*Nasal + stop
- \*Fricative + fricative
**Permitted final clusters (coda):**
- Liquid + stop: /ld, lt, rd, rt/
- Nasal + stop: /nd, nt, mp/
- Fricative + stop: /ft, st, št/
**Forbidden final clusters:**
- \*Stop + stop
- \*Pharyngeal + any
**ROOT CONSTRAINTS:**
1. **No identical consonants adjacent** (except in gemination across morpheme boundary)
   - ✗ \*K-K-B
   - ✓ K-T-B
2. **Pharyngeals typically not in same root**
   - ✗ \*Ḥ-ʿ-R
   - ✓ Ḥ-K-M
3. **Emphatics typically not in same root**
   - ✗ \*Ṣ-Ṭ-D
   - ✓ Ṣ-D-K
4. **Homorganic consonants avoided**
   - ✗ \*T-D-Ṭ (all alveolar)
   - ✓ T-R-K (varied places)
**VOWEL HARMONY:** None (explicitly rejected)
**CONSONANT HARMONY:** Weak tendency to avoid same-place articulations in roots

## 3. MORPHOLOGICAL SYSTEM
### 3.1 Root Structure
**SYSTEM OVERVIEW:**
Soulaani uses a **consonantal root system** where meaning resides in 2, 3, or 4 consonants, and grammatical function is expressed through vowel patterns (templates) intercalated between the root consonants.
**ROOT TYPES:**
| Type | Structure | Frequency | Example |
|------|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **Biconsonantal** | C₁C₂ | ~40% | **FK** (cultivate), **OK** (water) |
| **Triconsonantal** | C₁C₂C₃ | ~50% | **SKM** (power), **HKL** (understand) |
| **Quadriconsonantal** | C₁C₂C₃C₄ | ~10% | **KMPT** (compute), **LKTR** (electric) |
**This is the CORE MECHANIC:** All vocabulary generation must begin with root identification/creation.
### 3.2 Verb Patterns (Binyanim)
**TRICONSONANTAL VERB TEMPLATES:**
For root **C₁C₂C₃**, the following patterns exist:
#### **PATTERN I: CaC·iC·a (Basic Active)**
**Function:** Simple active verb, basic meaning of root
**Template:** C₁aC₂·iC₃·a
**Example (SKM):** sak·im·a = "to empower"
**Conjugation (Imperfective):**

### 3.6 AAVE Features — Structural Integration
**CRITICAL:** These are not "added on" — they are **structurally integrated** into the grammar.
#### **HABITUAL "BE" — bi- prefix**
**Function:** Marks habitual, ongoing, or characteristic action (AAVE "be")
**Structure:** bi·SUBJECT·VERB
**Examples:**
```
mi·kay·a         = I work (neutral)
bi·mi·kay·a      = I be working (habitual, "I regularly work")
ti·hak·ol·a      = you understand (neutral)
bi·ti·hak·ol·a   = you be understanding (habitual)
```
**Gloss:** "be" → bi-
---
#### **COMPLETIVE "DONE" — don particle**
**Function:** Marks completed action with current relevance (AAVE "done")
**Structure:** don SUBJECT·VERB·PERFECT
**Examples:**
```
hak·ol·ku           = I understood
don mi·hak·ol·ku    = I done understood (completed, perfective)
sak·im·sen          = they empowered
don sak·im·sen      = they done empowered (finished empowering)
```
**Gloss:** "done" → don
---
#### **REMOTE PAST "BEEN" — bin particle**
**Function:** Marks action in distant past (AAVE "been")
**Structure:** bin SUBJECT·VERB·PERFECT
**Examples:**
```
hak·ol·ku           = I understood
bin mi·hak·ol·ku    = I been understood (long ago)
ni·sik·em           = we are powerful
bin ni·sik·em       = we been powerful (ancestral power)
```
**Gloss:** "been" → bin
---
#### **IMMEDIATE FUTURE "FINNA" — f'na particle**
**Function:** Imminent action about to happen (AAVE "finna")
**Structure:** f'na SUBJECT·VERB
**Examples:**
```
mi·tay              = I come
f'na mi·tay         = I'm finna come (about to come)
ti·hak·ol·a         = you understand
f'na ti·hak·ol·a    = you finna understand (about to understand)
```
**Gloss:** "finna" → f'na
---
#### **DISTANT FUTURE — gon- infix**
**Function:** Future action (will, someday)
**Structure:** SUBJECT·gon·VERB
**Examples:**
```
mi·tay              = I come
mi·gon·tay          = I will come (someday)
ni·sik·em           = we are powerful
ni·gon·sik·em       = we will be powerful (future state)
```
**Gloss:** "will" → gon-
---
#### **NEGATION — en·..·š circumfix**
**Function:** Negative frame around verb (influences of Coptic/Arabic negation + AAVE "ain't")
**Structure:** SUBJECT·en·VERB·š
**Examples:**
```
mi·hak·ol·a             = I understand
mi·en·hak·ol·a·š        = I don't understand
ti·sik·em               = you are powerful
ti·en·sik·em·š          = you ain't powerful
```
**Perfective negation:** VERB·SUFFIX·š
```
hak·ol·ku               = I understood
hak·ol·ku·š             = I didn't understand
```
**Standalone negator:** an·š = "ain't"
```
Mi an·š har·a           = I ain't good
```
---
#### **COPULA PATTERNS**
**THREE COPULAS:**
1. **yit** = predicative (A = B in quality)
   - *Mi yit·sek·am·u* = "I am powerful-one"
2. **wi** = existential/locative (A exists/is located)
   - *Mi wi en·na·hawt* = "I am in the house"
3. **pe/te** = identificational (A IS B by identity)
   - *Anok pe na·soulaan* = "I AM the Soulaani"
**ZERO COPULA:** Also permitted in casual speech
```
Mi har·a        = I [am] good (copula dropped)
Ti yit·djak·iy  = You are intelligent (copula present)
```

## 4. GRAMMATICAL STRUCTURE
### 4.1 Word Order
**BASE WORD ORDER: SVO (Subject-Verb-Object)**
```
Mi  hak·ol·a  na·shey
I   understand the·thing
"I understand the thing"
```
**FLEXIBILITY:**
- **VSO** permitted for emphasis or questions:
  ```
  Hak·ol·a·ku  ti?
  understand·me you
  "Do you understand me?"
  ```
- **Topicalization** moves constituents to front:
  ```
  Na·shey, mi hak·ol·a·to
  the·thing, I understand·it
  "The thing, I understand it"
  ```
**QUESTION WORD ORDER:** Same as declarative (SVO) with rising intonation OR fronted question particle:
```
Ti hak·ol·a·ku?         = You understand me? (rising intonation)
Ko ti hak·ol·a·ku?      = Do you understand me? (question particle)
```
---
### 4.2 Agreement System
#### **SUBJECT-VERB AGREEMENT**
**Imperfective:** Subject prefix + optional plural suffix
| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 1st | mi- | ni- |
| 2nd | ti- | ti-...-tin |
| 3rd | i- | i-...-sen |
**Examples:**
- mi·hak·ol·a = "I understand"
- ti·hak·ol·a·tin = "y'all understand" (prefix + suffix)
**Perfective:** Person suffix only (no prefix)
| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 1st | -ku | -nun |
| 2nd | -ki | -tin |
| 3rd | -Ø | -sen |
**Examples:**
- hak·ol·ku = "I understood"
- hak·ol·tin = "y'all understood"
---
#### **NOUN-ADJECTIVE AGREEMENT**
**NO AGREEMENT REQUIRED**
Adjectives follow nouns without inflection:
```
na·shey har·a           = the thing good (the good thing)
na·pa·shey·w har·a      = the·PL·things good (the good things)
```
**Gender:** Not marked (no gender system)

### 4.11 Possession/Genitive
**POSSESSIVE SUFFIXES ON NOUNS:**
Same as object pronouns: -ku, -ki, -to, -nun, -tin, -sen
```

shey·nun        = our thing
shey·tin        = y'all's thing

```
**GENITIVE CONSTRUCTION (of):**
**Pattern 1:** POSSESSED + POSSESSOR (construct state-like)
```
shey·na·'an·ak·u
thing·the·person
"the person's thing" (lit. "thing of the person")
```

**Pattern 2:** POSSESSED + na + POSSESSOR (with particle)

```
shey na·'an·ak·u
thing of·the·person
"the thing of the person"
```
**Both patterns are valid; Pattern 2 is more common in longer phrases.**

### 5.2 Root Extraction Methodology
**STEP-BY-STEP PROCESS:**
#### **STEP 1: IDENTIFY CONCEPT**
Determine what concept needs a root. Be specific.
Example: "understand through hearing"
---
#### **STEP 2: SELECT SOURCE LANGUAGE(S)**
Use domain mapping:
- **Cosmological/philosophical?** → Egyptian
- **Agricultural/land?** → Hitchiti
- **Spiritual/legal?** → Semitic
- **Modern/tech?** → AAVE/English
- **Classical/abstract?** → Latin/Greek
Example: "understand through hearing" → **Muskogean** (traditional knowledge transmission)
---
#### **STEP 3: FIND SOURCE WORD**
Research the concept in the chosen source language.
Example: Hitchiti/Mvskoke *hakolo* = "to hear, to understand"
---
#### **STEP 4: EXTRACT CONSONANTAL ROOT**
**FOR SEMITIC SOURCES:** Already triconsonantal, extract directly
- Arabic *kataba* (wrote) → K-T-B
**FOR NON-SEMITIC SOURCES:** Identify stressed syllable and extract consonants
**Extraction Rules:**
1. **Identify core syllable(s)** containing the semantic weight
2. **Extract consonants only** (strip vowels)
3. **Prefer 2-3 consonants**; extend to 4 only for complex technical terms
4. **Apply Soulaani phonotactics**:
   - Convert sounds not in inventory to nearest equivalent
   - Avoid forbidden clusters
   - Respect homorganic constraints
**Example:**
```
Hitchiti: hakolo → ha-ko-lo
Core: ko-lo (semantic core)
Extract: K-L
BUT: also preserve H (important)
Final root: H-K-L (3 letters, preserves original structure)
```
**Example 2:**
```
English: computer → com-pu-ter
Core: com-put
Extract: K-M-P-T (4 letters, technical term)
```
**Example 3:**
```
Egyptian: mꜣʿt (ma'at) → already has consonants
Extract: M-ʿ-T (pharyngeal ʿ is in inventory)
```
---
#### **STEP 5: VERIFY UNIQUENESS**
**Check against existing root lexicon** (Section 6).
- Search for identical root
- Search for phonetically similar roots
- Check semantic domain overlap
**If collision detected:**
- Modify consonants slightly (e.g., K-L → H-K-L)
- Choose different source language
- Use 4-letter root to differentiate
---
#### **STEP 6: APPLY VOWEL TEMPLATES**
Generate full paradigm using established patterns:
```
Root: H-K-L
Verb (Pattern I):     hak·ol·a        = to understand
Abstract noun:        hik·ol·i        = understanding
Agent noun:           hek·ol·u        = understander
Patient noun:         hok·ol·on       = understood thing
Locative:             hak·ol·ow       = school
Adjective:            hak·ol·iy       = understanding (adj)
```
---
#### **STEP 7: DOCUMENT**
Add to lexicon with:
- Root consonants
- Source language + source word
- Gloss (1-3 words)
- Full paradigm
- Usage examples
---
### 5.3 Priority & Selection Criteria
**WHEN MULTIPLE SOURCES EXIST FOR ONE CONCEPT:**

1. **Does one source have deeper cultural significance?**

### 5.4 Collision Avoidance
**BEFORE CREATING A ROOT:**
1. **Search Section 6 (Current Root Lexicon)** for:
   - Exact match
   - Similar consonant sequence
   - Homonyms (same root, different meaning)
2. **Check phonetic similarity:**
   - Roots differing by one consonant may be too similar
   - Example: K-T-B vs K-D-B (avoid if possible)
3. **Check semantic overlap:**
   - If two roots mean nearly the same thing, choose one or differentiate by register/nuance
**RESOLUTION STRATEGIES:**
If collision detected:
**Option 1: Modify consonants**
- Original: F-K (cultivate)
- Collision with F-K (flex, from AAVE)
- Resolution: Use F-L-K for "flex" (4 letters) or F-L-S
**Option 2: Use different source**
- If Arabic S-L-M (peace) collides with something, try Hebrew Š-L-M or Greek form
**Option 3: Accept as homonym IF semantically distant**
- F-K (cultivate, agriculture) vs F-K (flex, show off) = different domains
- Solution: Track as FK₁ and FK₂, differentiate by context
**Option 4: Extend to 4-letter root**
- For complex concepts or to avoid collision
- Example: KMPT (compute) rather than KMT
